Thermalization dynamics in closed quantum many-body systems: A precision large-scale exact diagonalization study

If you mix a cold system with a hot system, energy will disperse over time until everything reaches a uniform temperature. Once you have reached this thermal equilibrium, you can't easily tell that the system was ever separated into hot and cold. That kind of information is "scrambled".

Now, take a system of many interacting quantum particles. Start the system in a specific, organized configuration with fixed total energy, and then let it evolve according to quantum mechanics. Does the system eventually reach a state that looks just like thermal equilibrium at that energy? This phenomena is what thermalization refers to.

This work provides very precise computational evidence of exactly how isolated quantum systems thermalize, validating what theory has predicted. We also explore thermalization beyond what is currently known from theory.

Key Results

  1. Thermalization happens: The systems do eventually look thermal, but with small deviations that are measured precisely.
  2. Deviations follow predictions: There's a theory called the "Eigenstate Thermalization Hypothesis" (ETH) that predicts exactly how these small deviations should behave, and our results match these predictions very well.
  3. Information scrambling: We compare how much information gets "lost" or "scrambled", and we find it matches what you'd expect from a thermal system.
  4. Relaxation dynamics: We observe that the system approaches equilibrium through an exponential decay process, with timescales that grow with the system size and depend on energy.
