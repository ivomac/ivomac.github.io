Conformal and chiral phase transitions in Rydberg chains

New experimental techniques let physicists build arrays of atoms suspended with lasers and magnetic fields, and manipulate how they interact with each other in a way that used to only be accessible through computer simulations. Recent experiments created chains of 51 atoms and saw them undergo phase transitions: sudden changes in the chain's quantum state.

Phase Transitions

Phase transitions are quantified by their critical exponents: a set of numbers that are universal, in that the same phase transition can be observed in many seemingly unrelated systems, and in each of them the same critical exponents will be measured. The particular phase transitions observed in this experiment are not fully understood, and their associated critical exponents (estimated from experimental measurements) are not knowne exactly.

Experimental Results

A crucial exponent of the phase transitions observed is the KZ exponent. The experimental results were:

For 1/3 phase: KZ ≈ 0.38. For 1/4 phase: KZ ≈ 0.25

A crucial element that can affect the accuracy of the exponent estimation is the size of the system, or the total number of atoms, 51 in this case.

Numerical approach

We used a powerful computational method (DMRG) to calculate these exponents and to understand what system sizes are needed to reach very high accuracy. This technique allows us to simulate quantum systems at system sizes much larger than 51.

A key step was to find the correct conditions in the system where complicating factors (like chiral perturbations) are reduced, to be able to study the more fundamental transition and its neighborhood.

Results

Our calculations estimate the exponents as:

For 1/3 phase: KZ ≈ 0.4545. For 1/4 phase: KZ ≈ 0.444

Both significantly higher than the experimental estimates. Our work provides precise references for the experimenters to calibrate their setups. We also show that with only 51 atoms, the experimental systems are too small to show the true phase transition behavior.
